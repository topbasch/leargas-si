About Léargas SI — methodological note

Léargas SI (Systems Intelligence) is a domain-specific analytical framework built on the foundation of the PESTLE methodology — refining it for energy system analysis. Where PESTLE applies six dimensions (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) to strategic assessment, Léargas SI replaces the generic Economic and Environmental lenses with a domain-calibrated Exergetic lens, which evaluates not just energy quantity but thermodynamic quality and recovery potential.

The result is a six-lens framework — Technical, Financial, Political, Ecological, Social, Exergetic — that retains PESTLE's structural familiarity while enabling analytically precise evaluation of energy infrastructure decisions. Where PESTLE asks "what is the environmental impact?", Léargas SI asks "what grade of energy is being wasted, and what is its recoverable economic value?" This distinction is analytically consequential in any system involving thermal flows.

This whitepaper constitutes the first published application of Léargas SI v2 to an Irish infrastructure case.

Section 1

The Structural Tension

EirGrid's Grid-Forming Strategy correctly identifies the inertia deficit created by retiring synchronous generators. As thermal generation exits the Irish system, the synthetic inertia needed for grid stability must be sourced from new mechanisms — battery storage, synchronous condensers, and demand-side flexibility. The strategy, however, addresses only the supply side of this transition.

Data centres represent a growing and systematically underanalysed dimension of this challenge. In 2023, data centres accounted for 20.1% of all Irish electricity demand, up from an estimated 14% in 2020 [1]. EirGrid projects this share reaching 31% by 2034 under current connection trajectories. This concentration creates a structural dependency: the grid's largest demand class operates largely outside the GFM mandate and contributes no thermal inertia to the system it stresses.

Simultaneously, Ireland's housing crisis concentrates population pressure on the Greater Dublin Area (GDA), where land costs and planning constraints prohibit affordable residential development at the required scale. The National Development Plan targets over 50% of population growth outside the Dublin core — yet the policy instruments to achieve voluntary, infrastructure-led decentralisation remain underspecified.

A third dimension is overlooked in both policy discussions: data centres operate at severe exergetic loss. Modern hyperscale facilities with Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) ratios of 1.2–1.5 convert 17–33% of consumed electrical energy into waste heat — discharged to atmosphere with no economic recovery. In liquid-cooled systems, this waste heat is available at 60–80°C: a temperature band directly usable in modern low-temperature district heating networks without additional upgrade [4].

Two structural failures — grid inertia deficit and housing concentration — are being managed as separate problems. They share a common asset: stranded thermal energy generated at scale, at the right locations, at the right temperature.
Section 2

The Core Insight: Two Crises, One Infrastructure

The conventional framing and its failure

The standard policy question asks: "How do we pipe Dublin's data centre waste heat to where housing demand exists?" This framing fails on physics and economics. District heating networks are economically unviable beyond approximately 15–40 km for large residential loads, due to pipeline heat losses and capital cost per connection. Dublin's housing demand is concentrated within the M50 ring; its data centres are migrating outward. The gap between demand concentration and heat source location is widening under the current policy trajectory.

The correct reframing

The productive question is the inverse: bring housing to the heat source. Data centres are already migrating to the GDA periphery — Kildare, Meath, Wicklow — driven by EirGrid's moratorium on new data centre connections in Dublin and by lower land costs outside the capital [5]. These are precisely the counties where the National Development Framework designates new residential growth zones. The spatial convergence is not coincidental. It is a structural alignment that existing policy has not yet activated.

Why this is a demand-side GFM mechanism

District heating supplied by data centre waste heat creates an aggregated, predictable thermal load at the data centre site. This load profile — large, geographically fixed, operationally continuous — is structurally compatible with demand-response participation in EirGrid's future GFM framework. A data centre operator with a thermal offtake obligation has an economic incentive to stabilise its own load profile, reducing curtailment events and improving grid compatibility. The thermal link converts a passive demand centre into a candidate flexibility asset.

Specific confirmed peripheral locations include: Meta Clonee (Co. Meath, 288 MW), Echelon DUB20 at Arklow (Co. Wicklow, planning approved, €3.5 billion campus), and multiple upcoming facilities in Kildare identified in the 2025 market database [3, 5]. All three counties appear in NDF regional growth designations.

Section 3

Six-Lens Assessment — Léargas SI v2

LensAnalysis
Technical District heating networks are technically viable within 5–15 km of GDA-peripheral data centres. Liquid-cooled systems supply heat at 60–80°C, compatible with modern low-temperature DH networks without additional heat pump upgrade — a significant capital cost advantage over lower-temperature industrial sources. The SEAI National Heat Study (2022) demonstrates that cost per MWh plateaus at heat densities above 1,000 MWh/km, not the previously assumed 10,000 MWh/km threshold, substantially expanding the viable deployment zone [4]. The Tallaght District Heating Network, operational since April 2023, confirms technical feasibility in the Irish context.
Key constraint: heat recovery equipment must be installed at the data centre; operator cooperation is required. Commercial arrangements remain to be standardised.
Financial Near-zero space heating cost — the primary household energy expenditure in Ireland, averaging €660–€1,320 annually for gas customers — constitutes a locational advantage that reduces effective housing cost without direct capital subsidy to residents. Pipeline capital expenditure of approximately €2M/km (SEAI NHS 2022, Appendix B [4]) is recoverable against heat tariff revenue within 4–12 years at standard commercial rates of €50–80/MWh, competitive against gas at approximately €120/MWh at 2025 market prices. The Tallaght precedent demonstrates that Amazon Web Services supplies waste heat at no monetary cost as a corporate sustainability commitment — a model that reduces operator-side financial barriers.
Key constraint: long-term heat supply agreements with data centre operators require regulatory underpinning not yet in place.
Political The proposal integrates into four active state workstreams without requiring new institutions. SEAI's District Heating Development Programme provides feasibility and pre-construction funding. IDA Ireland's peripheral siting incentives are already directing data centre investment to the relevant counties. The NDF designates Kildare, Meath, and Wicklow as primary growth corridors. The District Heating Steering Group (established under Climate Action Plan 2021, reporting annually to Government) explicitly recommended in its 2023 report that the Department of Housing strengthen DH references in the National Planning Framework [6]. The regulatory hook for thermal obligations on new data centre grid connections remains the most underdeveloped link — but the CRU has precedent for demand-side conditions in connection agreements.
Ecological Waste heat recovery reduces the thermodynamic inefficiency of data centre operation without changing IT output — a genuine efficiency gain rather than a substitution. The Tallaght scheme achieved 1,265 tonnes of CO₂ savings in its first operational year (2024) from a 32,800 m² heated area [6]. Scaled to a Clonee-equivalent thermal output, the CO₂ impact is proportionally larger. District heating also reduces reliance on oil — Ireland's dominant residential heating fuel, at 36% of household energy, the highest share of any EU member state except Cyprus [7]. Decarbonisation of heat is one of Ireland's most underperforming climate targets; this mechanism addresses it structurally rather than through individual retrofit subsidies.
SEAI Climate Action Plan 2024 targets 2.7 TWh of district heating by 2030 — current installed capacity is below 0.1 TWh [6].
Social The mechanism offers voluntary decentralisation incentivised by housing economics, not administrative direction. Peripheral housing with near-zero heating cost is locationally competitive with Dublin despite lower amenity — particularly for households where remote work reduces commute dependency. This does not solve Dublin's housing crisis but reduces the rate of demand concentration. The geographic equity dimension is significant: peripheral counties consistently show lower housing completion rates, lower rental yields, and higher fuel poverty rates than the GDA. A heating cost advantage distributed to new housing clusters addresses a dimension of geographic inequality that capital-subsidy programmes have failed to reach efficiently.
Exergetic This is the Léargas SI differentiator. Standard environmental analysis records waste heat as a cost — a PUE inefficiency to be minimised. Exergetic analysis asks: at what temperature grade is this energy available, and what is its maximum recoverable work value? At 60–80°C, data centre waste heat falls within the useful temperature band for space and water heating — its exergetic quality is matched to the application. Routing it to residential heating recovers economic value that atmospheric discharge destroys entirely. The Total Factor Productivity contribution is measurable: avoided imported gas per heated household, multiplied by the number of households served. At the Clonee scale, this represents a non-trivial reduction in Irish energy import dependency.
Exergetic quality assessment distinguishes this analysis from standard PESTLE: the mechanism is viable precisely because the waste heat temperature matches the application temperature — not all waste heat sources share this property.

Model boundary: These figures represent annual thermal ceiling at each parameter setting. Actual utilisation would be reduced by seasonal demand variation, network losses (~10%), and peak load sizing. A full engineering feasibility study would apply hourly demand profiles and diversification factors. These numbers answer the question "is the scale of opportunity meaningful?" — not "what should the system be sized to?"

Section 4

Existing Policy Hooks — No New Institutions Required

A common obstacle to cross-sectoral infrastructure proposals is institutional complexity. This proposal does not require new agencies, new legislation, or new funding instruments. Four active state workstreams already provide the hooks:

SEAI

District Heating Development Programme provides pre-construction feasibility funding and capital support. The National Heat Study (2022) explicitly identifies data centre waste heat as a priority resource and maps 20 data centre sites with thermal recovery potential clustered around Dublin. A National DH Centre of Excellence was recommended in 2023.

IDA Ireland

Active data centre siting policy already directs investment to GDA-peripheral counties. Thermal output obligations could be embedded in future site development agreements or investment incentive conditions — consistent with IDA's existing sustainability requirements framework.

CRU

As the grid connection regulator, CRU has precedent for attaching conditions to large demand connection agreements. A future GFM demand-side mandate could include thermal output obligations as a condition of connection — converting waste heat recovery from voluntary to structural.

NDF / LDA

The National Development Framework designates Kildare, Meath, and Wicklow as primary growth corridors for post-2030 housing. The Land Development Agency holds strategic sites in these counties. Thermal infrastructure availability is a legitimate input to site selection and viability assessment.

Section 5

Scope and Limitations

What this paper claims — and does not claim

✓The scale of opportunity at a single verified facility (Meta Clonee, 288 MW) is large enough to warrant formal feasibility assessment — this is demonstrated by the quantitative model.
✓The spatial alignment between data centre migration and NDP housing growth zones is real and currently underutilised in policy design.
✓Operational precedent (Tallaght, 2023) confirms technical and commercial feasibility at a smaller scale in the Irish context.
○This paper does not model hourly demand profiles, seasonal utilisation curves, or secondary distribution network costs. These require engineering-level feasibility studies.
○Heat supply agreement structures, operator compensation models, and consumer protection frameworks are outside this paper's scope. The Tallaght model (no-cost supply) may not be universally replicable at commercial scale.
○The Léargas SI exergetic lens quantifies the opportunity in thermodynamic and economic terms; it does not replace engineering thermodynamic analysis. PUE and recovery rate assumptions are indicative, not design parameters.
Section 6

Recommended Next Step

The case for a joint feasibility study is proportionate to the scale demonstrated. At the central scenario, a single GDA-peripheral data centre can supply recoverable thermal energy equivalent to the annual heating demand of 40,000–70,000 homes — a meaningful share of the NDP's peripheral housing growth targets for Meath and Kildare through 2040.

A joint feasibility study across SEAI, EirGrid, and IDA Ireland should:

— Map all GDA-peripheral data centre sites with confirmed or planned liquid-cooling infrastructure and quantify recoverable thermal output by temperature band
— Overlay thermal output potential against NDP housing growth zones at 5 km, 10 km, and 15 km proximity bands
— Model DH network costs against avoided grid balancing costs and household heating subsidies under Climate Action Plan scenarios
— Identify the CRU regulatory pathway for including thermal output obligations in future GFM demand-side connection requirements

Ireland's dual crisis — grid instability and housing unaffordability — shares a common structural root: the geographic and systemic misallocation of infrastructure value. Data centres produce exergetic waste at scale. Housing demand is concentrated where land is scarce. Aligning the two through thermal district heating infrastructure converts a dual liability into a dual solution — without requiring new institutions, new technologies, or public capital beyond feasibility investment. The question is not whether this is technically possible. The Tallaght scheme has already answered that. The question is whether the regulatory and commercial frameworks will be designed to activate it at scale.
